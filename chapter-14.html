Chapter 14: Semiconductor Electronics: Materials, Devices and Simple Circuits

               

1. Energy Band in Solids

       

Range of energy possessed by electrons in a solid.

       
            Valence Band: Range of energies of valence electrons
            Conduction Band: Range of energies of free electrons
            Forbidden Gap $E_g$: Energy gap between VB and CB        
       

Classification based on $E_g$:

       
            1. Conductors: $E_g = 0$, VB and CB overlap. Ex: Cu, Al
            2. Insulators: $E_g > 3eV$, Large gap. Ex: Diamond $E_g = 6eV$
            3. Semiconductors: $E_g \approx 1eV$, Small gap. Ex: Si $E_g = 1.1eV$, Ge $E_g = 0.7eV$        
       

2. Intrinsic Semiconductor

       

Pure semiconductor. No impurities added.

       
            At $T = 0K$: Behaves as insulator
            At $T > 0K$: Some $e^-$ jump to CB, leaving holes in VB
            $n_e = n_h = n_i$ where $n_i$ = intrinsic carrier concentration
            Conductivity: $\sigma = e(n_e\mu_e + n_h\mu_h)$        
       

3. Extrinsic Semiconductor

       

Doped semiconductor. Conductivity increases.

               

n-type Semiconductor:

       
            Doped with Pentavalent impurity: P, As, Sb
            Donor atoms, $n_e >> n_h$, Majority carriers = electrons
            Donor energy level just below CB        
       

p-type Semiconductor:

       
            Doped with Trivalent impurity: B, Al, Ga
            Acceptor atoms, $n_h >> n_e$, Majority carriers = holes
            Acceptor energy level just above VB        
       

4. p-n Junction

       

Formed when p-type and n-type semiconductors are fused together.

       
            Depletion Region: Region devoid of free charge carriers
            Potential Barrier: $V_0 \approx 0.7V$ for Si, $0.3V$ for Ge
            Barrier width $\approx 10^{-6}m$        
       

Forward Bias:

       
            p-side to +ve, n-side to -ve
            Depletion width decreases, Resistance low
            Current flows due to majority carriers
            Knee Voltage: $0.7V$ for Si, $0.3V$ for Ge        
       

Reverse Bias:

       
            p-side to -ve, n-side to +ve
            Depletion width increases, Resistance very high
            Only small reverse saturation current due to minority carriers
            Breakdown if $V_{reverse} > V_{breakdown}$        
       

5. p-n Junction Diode Applications

               

Half Wave Rectifier:

       
            Output: Pulsating DC
            Efficiency: $\eta = \frac{40.6}{1 + r_f/R_L}\%$
            Max efficiency = $40.6\%$
            Ripple factor: $\gamma = 1.21$        
       

Full Wave Rectifier:

       
            Efficiency: $\eta = \frac{81.2}{1 + r_f/R_L}\%$
            Max efficiency = $81.2\%$
            Ripple factor: $\gamma = 0.48$        
       

Zener Diode:

       
            Heavily doped p-n junction, works in reverse breakdown
            Used as voltage regulator
            Zener breakdown: Field ionization for $V_z < 6V$
            Avalanche breakdown: Collision for $V_z > 6V$        
       

6. Junction Transistor

       

3-layer semiconductor device: Emitter, Base, Collector

               

n-p-n Transistor:

       
            Emitter heavily doped, Base thin & lightly doped, Collector moderately doped
            Current relation: $$I_E = I_B + I_C$$
            $\alpha_{dc} = \frac{I_C}{I_E} \approx 0.98$
            $\beta_{dc} = \frac{I_C}{I_B} \approx 50-300$
            Relation: $$\beta = \frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha}$$, $$\alpha = \frac{\beta}{1+\beta}$$        
       

Transistor as Amplifier (CE mode):

       
            Current gain: $$\beta = \frac{\Delta I_C}{\Delta I_B}$$
            Voltage gain: $$A_v = \beta\frac{R_C}{R_B}$$
            Power gain: $$A_p = \beta^2\frac{R_C}{R_B}$$
            Phase difference: $180°$ between input and output
