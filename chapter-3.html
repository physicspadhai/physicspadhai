Chapter 3: Current Electricity

               

1. Electric Current

       

Rate of flow of electric charge through any cross-section of a conductor.

       
            $$I = \frac{q}{t} = \frac{dq}{dt}$$             SI Unit: Ampere (A)
            $1 A = 1 Cs^{-1}$
            Current is a scalar quantity but has direction of flow.        
       
            Convention: Direction of current is taken as direction of flow of positive charge, opposite to electron flow.        
       

2. Drift Velocity

       

Average velocity with which free electrons get drifted towards positive end under external electric field.

       
            $$v_d = \frac{eE\tau}{m}$$             Where $\tau$ = relaxation time
            Relation with current: $$I = neAv_d$$
            $n$ = number density of electrons, $A$ = area of cross-section        
       

3. Ohm's Law

       

At constant temperature, current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to potential difference across its ends.

       
            $$V \propto I$$ or $$V = IR$$             Where $R$ = Resistance
            SI Unit of R: Ohm ($\Omega$)
            $1 \Omega = 1 VA^{-1}$        
       
            Limitations: Ohm's law is not valid for semiconductors, diodes, electrolytes.        
       

Resistance and Resistivity

       
            $$R = \rho \frac{l}{A}$$             $\rho$ = Resistivity or Specific Resistance
            SI Unit of $\rho$: $\Omega m$
            Conductance: $G = \frac{1}{R}$, Unit: Siemens (S)
            Conductivity: $\sigma = \frac{1}{\rho}$, Unit: $Sm^{-1}$        
       

Temperature Dependence of Resistance

       
            $$R_t = R_0[1 + \alpha(t - t_0)]$$             $\alpha$ = Temperature coefficient of resistance
            For metals: $\alpha$ is positive, For semiconductors: $\alpha$ is negative        
       

4. Combination of Resistors

       

Series Combination:

       
            $$R_s = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + ...$$             Current same, Voltage divides: $V = V_1 + V_2 + V_3...$        
       

Parallel Combination:

       
            $$\frac{1}{R_p} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + ...$$             Voltage same, Current divides: $I = I_1 + I_2 + I_3...$        
       

5. Kirchhoff's Laws

       

First Law - Junction Rule:

       

Sum of currents entering a junction = Sum of currents leaving the junction.

       
            $$\sum I = 0$$             Based on conservation of charge.        
       

Second Law - Loop Rule:

       

Algebraic sum of potential changes in a closed loop is zero.

       
            $$\sum V = 0$$ or $$\sum IR + \sum EMF = 0$$             Based on conservation of energy.        
       

6. Wheatstone Bridge

       

Arrangement of four resistors used to measure unknown resistance.

       
            Balanced condition: $$\frac{P}{Q} = \frac{R}{S}$$             When galvanometer shows zero deflection, $V_B = V_D$        
       
            Note: Wheatstone bridge is most sensitive when all four resistors are of same order.        
       

7. Meter Bridge

       

Practical form of Wheatstone bridge to find unknown resistance.

       
            Unknown resistance: $$S = R\frac{(100-l)}{l}$$             Where $l$ = balancing length from left end        
       

8. Potentiometer

       

Device used to measure EMF of cell, internal resistance and to compare EMFs.

       
            Principle: $$V \propto l$$
            Comparison of EMFs: $$\frac{E_1}{E_2} = \frac{l_1}{l_2}$$
            Internal resistance: $$r = R\left[\frac{l_1 - l_2}{l_2}\right]$$        
       
            Advantage: Potentiometer measures actual EMF because it draws no current at balance point.        
       

9. Cells in Series and Parallel

       

Series Combination:

       
            $$E_{eq} = E_1 + E_2 + E_3...$$
            $$r_{eq} = r_1 + r_2 + r_3...$$
            Current: $$I = \frac{nE}{R + nr}$$        
       

Parallel Combination:

       
            For identical cells: $$E_{eq} = E$$
            $$r_{eq} = \frac{r}{n}$$
            Current: $$I = \frac{E}{R + r/n}$$        
       

10. Electrical Power

       
            $$P = VI = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R}$$             SI Unit: Watt (W)
            Electrical Energy: $E = Pt = VIt = I^2Rt$
            Commercial unit: 1 kWh = $3.6 \times 10^6$ J
