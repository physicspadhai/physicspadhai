Chapter 1: Electric Charges and Fields

               

1. Electric Charge

       

Charge is the intrinsic property of matter by virtue of which it experiences electromagnetic force. It is a scalar quantity.

       
            SI Unit: Coulomb (C)
            Dimensional Formula: $[AT]$
            Charge on electron: $e = -1.6 \times 10^{-19} C$
            Charge on proton: $+1.6 \times 10^{-19} C$        
               

Properties of Charge:

       
               
  • Additivity: Total charge = algebraic sum of all charges. $q = q_1 + q_2 + q_3 + ...$
    •            
  • Quantization: Charge exists in discrete packets. $q = \pm ne$ where $n = 1,2,3...$
    •            
  • Conservation: Total charge of an isolated system always remains constant.
    •        
       
            Note: Charge is invariant. It does not depend on speed of the particle.        
       

2. Coulomb's Law

       

The force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of charges and inversely proportional to square of distance between them.

       
            $$F = k \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$$             Where $k = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} = 9 \times 10^9 Nm^2C^{-2}$
            $\varepsilon_0$ = Permittivity of free space = $8.85 \times 10^{-12} C^2N^{-1}m^{-2}$        
       

In vector form: $$\vec{F}_{12} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \hat{r}_{12}$$

       
            Important: Coulomb's law is valid for point charges only and for distances > $10^{-15}$ m.        
       

3. Electric Field

       

Space around a charge where its influence can be felt by another charge. It is a vector quantity.

       
            Electric Field Intensity: $$\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q_0}$$             Due to point charge: $$\vec{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{q}{r^2} \hat{r}$$             SI Unit: $NC^{-1}$ or $Vm^{-1}$        
       

Electric Field Lines: Properties

       
               
  1. Start from positive charge and end on negative charge.
    2.            
  2. Never intersect each other.
    3.            
  3. Do not form closed loops.
    4.            
  4. Density of field lines represents strength of field.
    5.            
  5. Tangent at any point gives direction of $\vec{E}$.
    6.        
       

4. Electric Dipole

       

A system of two equal and opposite charges separated by a small distance.

       
            Dipole Moment: $\vec{p} = q \times 2\vec{a}$
            Direction: From negative to positive charge
            SI Unit: Coulomb-meter (C-m)        
       

Derivation: Electric Field on Axial Line of Dipole

       

Point P at distance r from center of dipole on axial line.

       
            $$E_{axial} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{2pr}{(r^2 - a^2)^2}$$             For $r >> a$: $$E_{axial} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{2p}{r^3}$$        
       

Derivation: Electric Field on Equatorial Line of Dipole

       
            $$E_{equatorial} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{p}{(r^2 + a^2)^{3/2}}$$             For $r >> a$: $$E_{equatorial} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{p}{r^3}$$        
       
            Relation: $E_{axial} = 2 \times E_{equatorial}$ for same distance.        
       

Torque on Dipole in Uniform Electric Field

       
            $$\vec{\tau} = \vec{p} \times \vec{E}$$             $$|\tau| = pE \sin\theta$$             Potential Energy: $U = -\vec{p} \cdot \vec{E} = -pE \cos\theta$        
       

5. Electric Flux

       

Total number of electric field lines passing normally through a surface.

       
            $$\phi_E = \vec{E} \cdot \vec{A} = EA \cos\theta$$             For closed surface: $$\phi_E = \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A}$$             SI Unit: $Nm^2C^{-1}$ or $V-m$        
       

6. Gauss's Law

       

Total electric flux through a closed surface is $\frac{1}{\varepsilon_0}$ times the total charge enclosed.

       
            $$\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{q_{enclosed}}{\varepsilon_0}$$        
       

Application 1: Field due to Infinitely Long Wire

       
            $$E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 r}$$             Where $\lambda$ = Linear charge density        
       

Application 2: Field due to Infinite Plane Sheet

       
            $$E = \frac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}$$             Where $\sigma$ = Surface charge density
            Note: E is independent of distance from sheet.        
       

Application 3: Field due to Thin Spherical Shell

       
            Outside ($r > R$): $$E = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{q}{r^2}$$
            On surface ($r = R$): $$E = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{q}{R^2}$$
            Inside ($r < R$): $$E = 0$$
