Chapter 5: Magnetism and Matter

               

1. Bar Magnet

       

A magnet having two poles - North and South. Monopole does not exist.

       
            Magnetic Dipole Moment: $\vec{M} = m \times 2\vec{l}$
            $m$ = pole strength, $2l$ = magnetic length
            Direction: South to North pole
            SI Unit: $Am^2$ or $JT^{-1}$        
       

2. Magnetic Field due to Bar Magnet

       

On Axial Line:

       
            $$B_{axial} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{2Mr}{(r^2 - l^2)^2}$$
            For $r >> l$: $$B_{axial} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{2M}{r^3}$$        
       

On Equatorial Line:

       
            $$B_{equatorial} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{M}{(r^2 + l^2)^{3/2}}$$
            For $r >> l$: $$B_{equatorial} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{M}{r^3}$$        
       
            Relation: $B_{axial} = 2 \times B_{equatorial}$        
       

3. Torque on Bar Magnet in Uniform B

       
            $$\vec{\tau} = \vec{M} \times \vec{B}$$             $$|\tau| = MB\sin\theta$$
            Potential Energy: $U = -\vec{M} \cdot \vec{B} = -MB\cos\theta$        
       

4. Gauss's Law in Magnetism

       

Net magnetic flux through any closed surface is always zero.

       
            $$\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{A} = 0$$             This implies magnetic monopoles do not exist.        
       

5. Earth's Magnetism

       

Earth behaves like a huge bar magnet with S pole near geographic north.

       
            Three elements:
            1. Declination ($\alpha$): Angle between geographic and magnetic meridian
            2. Dip/Inclination ($\delta$): Angle made by $B_{earth}$ with horizontal
            3. Horizontal Component: $B_H = B_E \cos\delta$
            Vertical Component: $B_V = B_E \sin\delta$
            $$B_E = \sqrt{B_H^2 + B_V^2}$$ and $$\tan\delta = \frac{B_V}{B_H}$$        
       

6. Magnetic Properties of Materials

       

Magnetisation Intensity:

       
            $$\vec{I} = \frac{\vec{M}}{V}$$ = Magnetic moment per unit volume
            SI Unit: $Am^{-1}$        
       

Magnetic Intensity:

       
            $$\vec{H} = \frac{\vec{B}_0}{\mu_0}$$
            $\vec{B}_0$ = Field due to external current only
            SI Unit: $Am^{-1}$        
       

Relation between B, H and I:

       
            $$\vec{B} = \mu_0(\vec{H} + \vec{I})$$        
       

Magnetic Susceptibility:

       
            $$\chi_m = \frac{I}{H}$$             Magnetic Permeability: $$\mu = \mu_0(1 + \chi_m)$$
            Relative Permeability: $$\mu_r = \frac{\mu}{\mu_0} = 1 + \chi_m$$        
       

7. Classification of Magnetic Materials

       
            1. Diamagnetic: $\chi_m$ small, negative. $\mu_r < 1$
            Ex: Cu, Bi, Pb, Water. Repelled by magnet.
                        2. Paramagnetic: $\chi_m$ small, positive. $\mu_r > 1$
            Ex: Al, Na, Ca. Feebly attracted.
                        3. Ferromagnetic: $\chi_m$ large, positive. $\mu_r >> 1$
            Ex: Fe, Ni, Co. Strongly attracted.
            Curie Law: $\chi_m \propto \frac{1}{T}$        
       

8. Hysteresis Loop

       

Lagging of I or B behind H for ferromagnetic material.

       
            Retentivity: Value of I when H = 0
            Coercivity: Value of H when I = 0
            Area of loop = Energy loss per cycle per unit volume        
       
            Soft Iron: Small coercivity, small area → Used in electromagnets, transformers
            Steel: Large coercivity, large area → Used in permanent magnets
